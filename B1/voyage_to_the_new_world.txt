The year was 1885, and the harbor of Bremen was filled with the smell of salt and coal smoke. Karl stood on the deck of the 'Atlantic Star', clutching his small wooden suitcase. He was only nineteen, and he was leaving everything he knew to find a new life in America. Around him, hundreds of other passengers were shouting goodbyes to their families on the dock. It was a moment of deep sadness, but also of great hope.

As the ship began to pull away, the land became smaller and smaller until it was just a grey line on the horizon. Karl shared a small, dark cabin with three other men. The air was thin, and the sound of the engine was always there, like a heavy heartbeat. However, the passengers spent most of their time on the upper decks. They shared stories of what they expected to find in the "New World." Some dreamed of owning large farms, while others wanted to work in the growing cities of New York and Chicago.

After ten days at sea, a violent storm hit the ship. The waves were as tall as houses, and the 'Atlantic Star' tossed like a toy in a bathtub. Karl stayed in his bunk, listening to the wood creak and the wind howl. He was terrified that he would never see land again. He thought of his mother's fresh bread and the quiet hills of his village. During those long hours, he realized that courage wasn't the absence of fear, but the decision to keep going despite it.

Finally, on the fourteenth morning, the cry of "Land ho!" rang out across the ship. The storm had passed, and the sun was shining on the Statue of Liberty. Karl felt a lump in his throat as he saw the giant figure holding her torch high. He didn't know what his future held, or if he would ever be successful. But as he stepped onto the dock at Ellis Island, he knew that his journey had changed him. He was no longer a boy from a village, but a man ready to build a new world.
